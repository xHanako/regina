Welcome to the Regina Angelorum convent church, one of the oldest and most unique temples in the Colonial City of Santo Domingo.

This enclosure, located on the corner of Padre Billini and José Reyes streets, was the first female convent established in the island and one of the earliest cloistered spaces for nuns in the New World. Its history dates back to the mid-sixteenth century, in the founding years of the city.

After the arrival of the first male religious orders – Franciscans, Mercedarians and Dominicans – the need arose to create female communities that would offer spiritual and social protection to young women and widows in the neighborhood. In 1556, a group of notable neighbors asked King Philip II to found a monastery of Dominican nuns. In response, in 1560 the first six nuns arrived in Santo Domingo, and shortly afterwards the construction of this temple, dedicated to Regina Angelorum or Mary, Queen of the Angels, was ordered.

The construction was possible thanks to the extraordinary generosity of María de Arana, a pious Spanish lady and widow of a wealthy landowner from El Seibo, who sold her goods and donated her fortune to build the church and convent. His gesture forever marked the history of Regina Angelorum.

Now look up at one of the most extraordinary elements of the temple: its brick dome, in Renaissance-Baroque style, raised on pendentives and pierced by skylights that allow natural light to pass through. Regina Angelorum is one of only two colonial churches in Santo Domingo that have a dome, along with the old church of the Jesuits, today the National Pantheon, which makes it an exceptional architectural piece within the colonial complex.

Notice also the high wooden choir, which was accessed directly from the convent. This structure is original from the colonial period and is considered the oldest preserved wooden choir in America. From here, the cloistered nuns participated in the liturgical celebrations, when they did not do so through the Mudejar-inspired latticework, maintaining the privacy of conventual life.

In this convent lived women of great intellectual and spiritual sensitivity. Among them stands out Sister Leonor de Ovando, considered one of the first Creole poets of the American continent, along with Elvira de Mendoza. Their early voices constitute an invaluable legacy of the written culture on the island.

En este convento vivieron mujeres de gran sensibilidad intelectual y espiritual. Entre ellas destaca sor Leonor de Ovando, considerada una de las primeras poetisas criollas del continente americano, junto a Elvira de Mendoza. Sus voces tempranas constituyen un legado invaluable de la cultura escrita en la isla.

Turn your attention now to the high altar. The temple has two remarkable Baroque wooden altarpieces, the main one being dedicated to the invocation of the Queen of Angels. In it there is a Marian image of dress, typical of the Spanish eighteenth century. The front of the altar, made of embossed silver, is one of the most refined pieces of the set. According to tradition, during the invasion of the city by the English pirate Francis Drake in 1586, the nuns painted this silver frontal black to hide its value and prevent it from being looted, demonstrating genious and bravery in times of danger.

The story of Regina Angelorum is also deeply linked to Father Francisco Xavier Billini, a priest of Italian origin and a central figure of Dominican charity in the nineteenth century. In 1877, after the discovery of the remains of Christopher Columbus in the Primate Cathedral of America, and while the mausoleum that would permanently house them was being built, Father Billini guarded and protected these remains in this church, safeguarding them in the crypt where, years later, he himself would be buried.

But Regina Angelorum is not just a monument of the past: it is a living church, deeply integrated into the spiritual life of the city. It is also one of the favorite churches for brides. Dedicated to the Marian invocation of the Immaculate Conception, many couples who marry here do so with faith and the hope of receiving the blessing of their children. And, as those who return affirm with emotion, that blessing comes.

Today, under the custody of the Order of Malta, Regina Angelorum continues to be a space of deep spiritual recollection. Here, stone, wood and brick come together to create an atmosphere of sobriety and beauty, permeated by centuries of prayer, faith, fulfilled promises and new life.

Before you head out, take a moment to gaze at this temple in silence. Regina Angelorum is not only a testimony of the past, but a place where history, faith and hope continue to meet.
